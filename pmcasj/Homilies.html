Introduction

Today's readings contain themes of seeing, healing, courage to witness, and to persevere. The first reading emphasizes spiritual seeing and the art of active listening in order to discern. The Gospel contains a healing by an action of Jesus and washing the one healed carrying overtones of baptism. Samuel is sent to the house of Jesse to select the king from his sons. On seeing the fine sons of Jesse he thought that the Lord's anointed must be before him. God taught him otherwise, "Not as man sees does God see, because man sees the appearance, but God looks into the heart." Samuel must wait the Lord's inner voice telling him of the Lord's choice. All of Jesse's sons were not present; the youngest was tending sheep on the mountain (God's traditional dwelling). Upon David's entry into the house, the Lord said, "There --anoint him, for this is the one." With the oil Samuel anointed David in the presence of his brothers and the Holy Spirit rushed upon David. The Gospel reading contains the healing of a man blind from birth and the action to carry out the miracle. The man follows Jesus' instructions and receives faith to worship Jesus and the courage to witness on his behalf against his enemies.

Gospel

There are longer and shorter readings of today's Gospel; since the longer form is 41 verses, I suspect that at most Masses the shorter form will be read, and so it is that about which I will write here.

The basic story is one of healing; it is in its simplest form a 'miracle-story': the need is presented to Jesus, who heals and then is praised or criticized for his curing. Most often what is stressed in a miracle-story is the power of Jesus; little is said about the love behind his choice to cure. Recall that what we have here is a reduction of the original event to a story form, when so often details we would like to have are not preserved, since they are not considered essential to the story's purpose in telling. A rarity, seen in this story, is the cure, not by word, but by action. No doubt the ancient world thought that healing powers belonged to clay (akin to our mud baths); saliva was simply necessary where there was no water easily available. Some interpreters think that we have here with the use of clay a belief in magic, such as that practiced in healing centers throughout the Mediterranean at this time by 'magicians'. But since Jesus almost always cures by word alone, it seems better to think that his use of clay is a symbol of the spiritual blindness with which one lives without faith in Christ. It seems that also necessary was the washing of the eyes in water - here the Pool of Siloam (located in the southwestern edge of Jerusalem). Given that symbolism is a major characteristic of John's Gospel, one concludes that this washing is a symbol of baptism, by which one is cured from sin and given life through the presence of the Holy Spirit. This role of the Holy Spirit, as giver of life, is greatly emphasized in John's Gospel, as in the Pauline literature; the Spirit remains the continued divine presence earlier experienced in the person of Jesus Christ. The miracle story eventually becomes a conflict story: a literary presentation of argumentation about some facet of the miracle. Here, it is the 'Jews' against the cured man; the story now resembles a trial, informal, but still before authority. [It should be understood that, in this Gospel, 'Jews' almost always means the opposition of certain Jewish authorities to Jesus, whether in his public life or in the later life of the community of believers; thus, 'Jews' does not mean all Jews.] The particular argument offered by the Jewish authority of the moment is that the cure worked by Jesus was done on the Sabbath, when one is not allowed to work; healing was considered a work and thus not to be done on the Sabbath. Jesus then is a sinner to this authority. Typical of John's Gospel is the appeal to fact: 'all I know is that I was blind and now see; who in our history has ever made the blind to see?' Given that the return of sight is one of the chief promises of Yahweh throughout the OT for his people, and given that such a benefit will characterize the Messiah (whenever he comes and whoever he is), it is not surprising that the newly cured man confesses Jesus to be Messiah - what else could the fact of this cure mean? The blind man is a symbol of that testimony in which the Gospel places its trust; he was, as Jewish authority claimed, 'born in sin from birth' [that is, he was blind from birth and this was a result of sin, Jews thought], and so should not be trusted. But the fact is the fact, the cure is the cure, and faith follows upon it, as the Gospel insists. That Jesus presents himself as Son of Man is a claim that he will be the Judge of the world, separating sinners from the obedient. On the basis of the cure, the man believes that Jesus is this Son of Man; unfortunately, the 'Jews' do not; they think they are safe from his final judgment.

Moses did as the Lord commanded and immediately there flowed out clear water for the people to drink. The flowing water that preserves life saved the Israelites who tested God at Meribah. In the Gospel a tired and loving Jesus still desires to give the living water of life to his people.

Gospel

We will comment on the longer (very long) form of this Sunday's Gospel, usually called the story of "The Woman at the Well". This story occurs as one of the seven signs of John's Gospel, signs which are meant to increase faith in Jesus and so secure eternal life. A first circumstance offered the reader is that this event occurs in Samara. Geographically, this means Jesus is somewhere between Galilee, where most all of his public life was spent according to the Synoptics, and Judaea, with its capitol Jerusalem, the place "where all the prophets are put to death". Also, the story centers on a well, and drawing water. Finally, besides Jesus, the 'other' character of the story is a woman who has come for water at noon. These details describe something unusual. That Jesus is so fully immersed in Samaria is something new to Gospel readers; that he will address a woman when the two of them are alone is not customary at all; that the woman draws water at noon suggests what is made clearer in the later story: because of her marital situation(s) she cannot draw water at the time this is usually done by the women of Sychar. With the disciples away to get some food, Jesus asks the woman for a drink of water. The woman lodges an objection which is only answered much later in the story: Jews and Samaritans are mortally enemies, and have been ever since the Samaritans began to exist, for despite Samaritans' claims to be the true children of Jacob, Israel considered them heretics, like pagans. In theory and in practice Samaritans are to be shunned. Apparently ignoring the woman's protest, Jesus, in the next few verses speaks about a water he could give her if only she asked it of him. (It is necessary here to be clear about 'water'; well water was brackish, the left-over from the Mediterranean rains. Good was the water that was fresh. Everyone wanted flowing or 'living' water; by necessity people had to settle for well water.) The water Jesus promises is the Holy Spirit, the giver of life; this the evangelist will make clear only in a later chapter. The woman tries to grasp the identity of Jesus: is he greater than the forefather who provided this well? Again, Jesus ignores the Christological question of his identity, promising the wonders of the 'water' he can give to one who believes in him. This part of the story which talks symbolically of the Spirit ends with the woman not really understanding the words of Jesus, but to the believer is assured that presence of the Spirit ('a spring of water) who brings one to eternal life. John now changes theme: he will note the distinction between pre-Jesus worshipers and Jesus' followers. The first group had their own places of worship, a mountain of Samaria and the mountain topped by Jerusalem. From a Jewish point of view, represented by Jesus, the Samaritans worship what 'they do not know' and Jews worship Yahweh of the Old Testament. But the day has arrived when Christians will worship God in Spirit and truth. "Spirit" here refers to the presence of the Spirit, the 'spring of water', who will guide Christian worship, a worship which is true, for it is the believer in Christ who has the truth about God. Such words from Jesus draw the statement, 'these are words of the Messiah, not simply of a prophet. For the first time, Jesus testifies here that indeed he is the Messiah. Again, the scene changes to a new focus. While the woman still ponders whether Jesus be the Messiah (something John's readers already know and believe), Jesus is pictured as looking over ripe Samaritan fields, ready for a harvest. He now describes himself as one who wants to do his Father's will; he calls this 'wanting' his real food. He foresees the harvesting which has culminated, by Gospel time, in the faith of many in himself; indeed, John will insist that very many have come to faith in Jesus, so that the Johannine community can understand not only its own role in preaching Jesus, but the fact that it is the result of the preachings of many early Christians. You might have guessed that John, while narrating an event of the30's AD, is really looking at the whole story from the point of view of Christians in 90'sAD. Jesus seems to be talking not only to people of his own time, but to the Johannine community now reading Jesus' story. Finally, John reports the fact that, once the Samaritan citizens met Jesus, who stayed with them for three days (not said by the Synoptics), they no longer needed to hear the testimony of the woman about Jesus; meeting him directly moved them believe in him. They call him 'Savior of the world'. As happens in most stories within Chapters 2-12, John makes sure that the Christological titles used in the 90's AD appear in the Gospel. While it is doubtful that people of Jesus' time understood fully these titles, by 90AD they did, and so John inserts them in their full sense into a story where people grasp the meaning of Jesus only in a limited sense. We should note that we hear nothing again about the Samaritan woman, and that we meet up with a Christian mission to Samaria only in Chapters 8 and 9 of Acts. John is more than willing to slide that later mission into his Gospel time, for the Gospel time was to bear the fruit of this later Samaritan mission.

Introduction

It is not unusual for God to give one a great need accompanied by the desire to fulfill his need. Abraham's was just such a case. He and his wife were childless and had a great desire to be parents. God would help and asked Abraham to leave his father's house and the land of his kinsfolk for a land the Lord will show him. Abraham obeyed without question at that time. The Lord would make a great nation of him and bless him among the communities of the earth. The test to challenge Abraham's faith came when God asked him to sacrifice his only son Isaac, thus eliminating any future natural progeny. Abraham was equal to the task and became a man transformed, our father in faith.

Gospel

This Gospel narrates the Transfiguration of Jesus on an unspecified mountain top (a favorite place of revelation for Judaism and other religions). That Peter was chosen to witness this transfiguration is one more sign of his importance in the Christian communities, especially after they grew once Jesus was gone. Why James and John are present is not so clear, though John is the beloved disciple (if one accepts that identification. The importance of John and James is made clear from the Acts of the Apostles; they are leaders of the Jerusalem Christian community, the foundation community, and are most important witnesses in that book of the truths about Jesus. Thus, they, like Peter, grew in importance from the earliest Christian times. The two concrete qualities that manifested the transfiguration are a 'face that shone like the sun' and 'clothes white as light'. The transfiguration itself is not explained. The Jewish psyche would immediately recognize the appeals to sun and light as characteristics of the Divine. In a Gospel which has witnessed to the divinity of Jesus as born, it is most significant that now the person of Jesus is once again affirmed to be divine. Further, the affirmation of Jesus' divinity at birth is intended to be a guide to interpreting the entire life of Jesus to be described in the Gospel. Similarly, there is purpose in revealing him so dramatically as divine here, and that purpose is to leave with the disciples the memory of the real identity of Jesus as he is very soon is about to undergo his Passion. One must never forget in the horrific treatment of Jesus that he is God. It is understood that Moses and Elijah are to be interpreted as the pillars of prophecy, Moses for the Law and Elijah for the Prophets. It is Luke who makes clear the point that these two august figures are discussing with Jesus his coming Passion; it is in the Law and Prophets that one will find the plan that the Messiah abandon for the moment his power and innocence and wisdom so as to undergo the cross. It is understandable that the disciples want to stay with Jesus as he is revealed to be their God; also understandable is the note that it is Peter speaks the mind of the disciples - Peter who is very outspoken and who is, and will be, the chief spokesman of the earliest witnesses to Jesus. Their desire to have Jesus stay represents the desire of the Christian communities of Matthew's experience to have Jesus with them always. 'Tents' is a symbol reference to what the OT knows to be 'the good old times' of simple life in the desert with Yahweh. All is interrupted by God's words from the symbol of cloud. These are the same words as heard at Jesus' baptism. There they spoke of a son who was most pleasing because of his devotion to execute the divine plan; here they are directed to his particular obedience to the will of God that he die. Added to the saying of God are the words, "Listen to him", which is a call to take seriously Jesus' various predictions of his Passion. As was true throughout the ancient world, to hear the words of God will strike fear in the heart: one is never sure why God intervenes in human life, and that uncertainty makes the human being fearful. The cloud, symbol in the OT of the presence and movement of God, leaves. It falls to Jesus to strongly urge his disciples to say nothing of the experience they have witnessed till after his resurrection. This is a subtle way of saying that only when all the experiences of Jesus's life were put together and meditated upon, did the disciples truly begin to understand all about Jesus. Before the resurrection they never understood the deepest reality of Jesus, particularly could they make no sense of the suffering of one who was for many months so powerful, wise and close to God.

Introduction

Today's readings present to us Adam and Eve in the Garden with the Serpent and Jesus in the desert with Satan. In the first reading the man and woman fail mankind; in the Gospel Jesus redeems the human race. The Lord God formed man from clay and blew into his nostrils the breath of life. Man became a living human being; at death we will return God's gift to him. God planted a garden with trees delightful to look at and good for food. The man and woman could eat of the trees in the garden except from the tree of knowledge of good and evil. To partake of its fruit was to disobey God, gain knowledge, and to die. The first and second took place immediately; disobedience alienated them from God and they now knew that they were naked. Their wisdom, shame and fear, knowing good and evil. They were dead to God (spiritual death) and would eventually die a natural death --the effects of sin. How can man redeem himself? He cannot; the creature is of the earth, he is not divine. Having rejected the gift of God's life, man, of himself, cannot become what he is not. Only God can save mankind; only God can forgive, but to forgive is not to redeem! Today's Gospel reflection gives us some idea of the work and meaning of redemption by Jesus Christ.

Gospel

Jesus' three temptations in the desert, according to Luke. Exegetes look at this episode from two angles: the meaning of the story in itself, then the meaning of the story in the full Gospel. We look at the story from these two perspectives. Jesus goes into the desert (presumably that territory east of the Jordan River where he was baptized). The best suggestion is that Jesus went there to reflect on the experience he had, the coming of the Spirit upon him and the voice from Heaven, "You are my beloved Son, my beloved." Jesus, we think, understood this experience to be his calling to be the person described in Isaiah's four songs about "the Servant of God". This servant, Isaiah says, is to preach repentance at first to Israel, then to the nations; thereafter, he, though innocent, will suffer death so that the guilty, who should die for their sins, may go free, forgiven. With this background we read the temptations of Satan against the completion of these tasks of God's servant, also called 'My beloved Son'. (Here, that God calls Jesus "My son, the beloved", stresses Jesus' filial obedience, though, as Luke has already argued in the Annunciation, Jesus will be born of the Spirit and Power from on High, and so will be called divine Son of God. Obedience, not divinity, is stressed now. The three responses to Satan's temptations are the teaching of this episode. They are offered to a Satan who does not wonder who Jesus is, but what might move him to back away from the role of Servant. First, to Satan's suggestion that Jesus will find food by his power, Jesus says that to attain true life, unending life, one must and will obey the word of God. No human power/wealth, but only obedience to the will of God will merit eternal life. To Satan's offer of an entire kingdom if Jesus will adore him, Jesus responds with the OT core statement: Only God (Yahweh) do I adore. One may esteem people greatly, but only one deserves adoration, the Creator of the creature; no one takes God's place, and the creature, as creature, must adore; such is the nature of being a creature. Finally, to Satan's suggestion that Jesus test God's love for him by putting himself in mortal danger, Jesus affirms that he will not tempt God, because he has no need to prove God's love for him. This is a statement of total trust in God's unfailing love, no matter what the circumstances (e.g. crucifixion) may be in Jesus' future. This Lucan narrative serves the entire Gospel in that it reveals, before Jesus' public life begins, what are the essential principles that guide his every choice: obedience to what God wants, adoration of God alone, absolute confidence in God's love for him always. Luke means to say, then, that, in a world not accustomed to psychology as ours is, the reader must always carry with him this understanding of the workings of Jesus' mind and will. Jesus will aver to one or other of these principles in his public life, particularly when he notes that he has been sent to announce the presence of the Kingdom of God and that he has come to call sinners. Most important is the revelation of his mind in the Garden, when, faced with ugly death, he asks his Father to save him, but that he will drink this bitter cup if this is his Father's will. And he will obey and worship in absolute trust that God's love is even in this crucifixion.

Introduction

Today's reading deal with the relationship between God and mankind, and how mankind is weak on his responsibilities and his understanding of God. "Zion said, 'The Lord has forsaken me; my Lord has forgotten me.'" Really? Out of consideration this "forgetful" Lord maintains his response within the created world and asks: "Can a woman forget her infant; be without tenderness for the child of her womb?" Think about it, such is her love. Even if she were to forget, "I will never forget you." God will not, cannot forget his child. We belong to him, we are part of him and his person as a human being in Jesus Christ. As we see in today's Gospel, He loves his creation and cares for it.

In the light of this truth about the source of happiness and the obedience or service to achieve it, Jesus will speak of God as only source of the happiness for which we long. Jesus' preference for Yahweh over Riches comes here not from argument, but from his undeniable wisdom: he urges the disciple to "seek first the kingdom of God and all these things will be given you besides (that kingdom)."

Gospel

Life is about more than food and the body about more than clothing. Jesus concluded there is more to life than food. What kind of life and what kind of food had he in mind? He refers them to his creatures --they obey God's commands. Look at the birds; they don't sow or reap, but your heavenly Father feeds them, and you are worth so much more to him. The flowers are dressed as beautifully as Solomon in all his glory, yet, at the end of the day they are burned in the furnace. How much more will he care for you, "You of little faith?"

Isaiah dealing with the feelings of abandonment by Zion, and Jesus dealing with the lack of trust in his disciples, address the same issue -- lack of faith. By becoming aware of our lack of trust and knowledge of God, we are meant to understand Jesus who is God's Beloved Son and a human man. Jesus, himself, had to grow in awareness of God's love for humanity and his creation. Obviously, as part of the Father's plan, Jesus knew that God loves his creatures, but he had to see and experience as a human, God's love for humanity. This love of God for man, realized by man, is motivation for man to choose to serve God and not Mammon. The birds must search for their food; man must obey God's commands. The animals obey God instinctively; man obeys freely, and with God's grace willingly. The birds forage for their food in their environment; mankind is given his food through receiving the sacraments of the Church --the environment of God in the world. I will never forget you, but we must seek actively the Food of Life, "You of little faith," that we be clothed in robes of glory.

In the Book of Leviticus the Lord God instructs Moses to tell the community of Israel to be holy, for the Lord God is holy. The holy person loves as God loves -- He loves all. If God is perfect, and he is holy, then we ought to be perfect as our heavenly Father is perfect. The person who is holy comes closest to loving as our heavenly Father loves.

Gospel

In this Gospel reading we are given the final two antitheses of the Sermon on the Mount; these antitheses, each in its own way, differ significantly from the teaching of the authoritative teachers of Jesus' time. Thus, it is a matter of who understands God's will best, whose wisdom best guides human life. The first antithesis we hear presents the biblical OT teaching, 'an eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth'; the second, 'you shall love your neighbor.' For a better explanation of the first 'eye for an eye', I should start with the second 'you shall love'. The command to love neighbor is never joined in the OT with the command to hate one's enemy; that fuller statement is a human effort to make sure the Israelite has nothing to do with an enemy (especially with one who does not respect the Mosaic Law) and thus to limit one's obligation to love one's enemy. Thus, it is lawful to hate a Samaritan, who is known to deny much of God's action in Israel's favor; Jesus' parable of the Good Samaritan flies in the face of this interpretation of God's will about love of neighbor. Jesus' teaching about God's will insists on love of both neighbor and enemy. The explanation for this teaching is God's own love of His enemies: He provides life for them instead of death, for He provides the essentials which keep them alive, which are sun and water. Being a child of a parent means that one will imitate that parent; one looks to God's actions towards His enemies to learn what, of one wants to be a child of God, must imitate. To help only one's friends is to do what sinners do, those who have no respect for God; one cannot live only with this kind of love. The question of importance here is: Who is my neighbor? Paul asked it of his readers, and the authoritative teachers asked it of Jesus (and of each other). Is one obliged to love a pagan; if not, then a sinful Jew? Is it not better to say one's obligation to love extends only to the virtuous, for whom one should be willing to suffer? Jesus says love extends to all, for the Israelite loves as God loves - which means 'all'. To love only a portion of God's human creatures is to be imperfect; to be perfect, like God, one loves all. To be 'perfect', 'complete' is the meaning of shalom; thus, be 'shalom', be perfect and complete, like God. With this understanding of the final antithesis one interprets the previous antithesis (and indeed all antitheses). Jesus differs from other teachers in his application of the essential command 'love God and love neighbor'. Jewish teachers agreed with Jesus about the centrality of this divine command. The antithesis we look at now offers four examples (resistance, cloak as well as tunic, the 'extra mile', giving to the borrower) to consider. None of these examples are to be raised to the level of universal principle; they are only examples, though of a deeper and universal principle of morality. (Thus, the non-resistance movement, though founded in part on the words of this antithesis, is not correct if in all circumstances, non-resistance is 'the moral right thing to do'; e.g. one cannot practice, as the will of Jesus, the law of non-resistance if one, while President of a nation, refuses to resist the attacking enemy - such a resistance denies the law of love neighbor and so cannot be considered to be the teaching of Jesus. Non-resistance is recommended only to the degree that it fosters love of neighbor.) Again, should one seek payment of a debt by taking property (i.e. a tunic - the undergarment), the debtor should also offer his outer garment (i.e. a cloak). This action manifests a justice from the debtor, who owes much to the kindness of the lender. Again, circumstances will dictate when and how such a kindness to a lender fulfills the only law that really matters: love of neighbor. Another example of this antithesis is 'going another mile'. It was the law in Israel that one was obliged to carry a soldier's burden (a Roman soldier was an enemy to these Israelites) one mile, provided he be carrying out a military order and the burden was job-related; no doubt, this law was greatly abused. An example of this law was the forcing Simon from Cyrene, Africa, to carry Jesus' cross to Calvary. Hateful as it was to be made to carry the enemy's kit, Jesus urges one to offer to go an extra mile - provided that, as always, one is thereby offering love to an enemy. Jesus on the Mount presents the will of God anew, i.e. with the perfect interpretation of that will. Unfortunately, Jesus had to take issue with contemporary interpretations (particularly those of Pharisees, Scribes and Sadducees); he did not disagree with all that Pharisees/Sadducees/Scribes taught, but his disagreements with them were sufficient to have them want him dead. Thus, Matthew presents first a Jesus who makes clear that he is not out to change the Law or to suppress it; after all, it is the wisdom of God and not to be abused. This Gospel reading is part of a 3-chapter account of Jesus' Sermon on the Mount. Matthew is unique and famous for 5 major addresses of Jesus, which are so placed in the Gospel as to seem to intentionally divide the Gospel into more or less equal parts. The Sermon on the Mount, the first of these 5 discourses, offers a substantial presentation of Jesus' moral teaching, i.e. his wisdom; his wisdom, the Gospel shows, is the most accurate understanding of the mind of God known to human beings in the moral sphere. After Jesus' famous beatitudes, the Lord turns to two metaphors by which to describe those who believe in him (which means they accept his moral understanding of God's commands). These two metaphors are salt and light; by means of them, Jesus means to describe his church. Salt is a commodity quite precious in Jesus' Israel; human beings know it gives life and taste. Their deeds can overcome moral death, foolishness, deceit and hate. Thus, with a few lines Jesus reveals the glory of his followers: salt to a world tending ever toward lifelessness and tastelessness (meaninglessness) and light to a world for its life and love and wisdom, to know how to live and whom to love.

Gospel

We are given a long Gospel reading, or a shorter version of it. I will comment on the longer version which includes the material of the shorter version. The Gospel presents the story of the Presentation of Jesus, and the Purification of Mary. Luke treats Mary's purification briefly. She, like all new mothers, was obliged to be purified, i.e. declared 'clean' by an official (a priest in the Jerusalem Temple). Her impurity was that in childbirth she had emitted blood and other liquids, all of which, as was the understanding of medicine at the time, could make a person ill or dead. Such a spilling of inner liquids must keep a person from association with other Israelite groups, especially from the women gathered in the women's section of the Jerusalem Temple for communal worship of Yahweh. Purification, however, was only one obligation imposed by Mosaic Law; a second was that a new-born male was to be offered to God 40 days after his birth (females 80 days). This is not circumcision, the symbol of belonging to the Chosen People; this offering means to remind Israel that God had let the Angel of Death kill every first-born Egyptian male (animal as well as human) in order to free Israel from Egypt. The first-born of Israel were saved from this slaughter by putting the blood of a lamb on their doorposts and lintels, thus signaling to the Angel that the first-born of this household should live by God's mercy. Jesus, a first-born (called that though Mary had no other children), had to be offered in recognition of this wonderful saving event; animals had to be sacrificed in the Jerusalem Temple area, but humans could not be put to death, so an animal (pigeons or turtledoves for the poor) would be sacrificed in the place of the son. Added to Simeon's interpretation of Jesus is his prophecy that Jesus will be a subject of controversy, suggesting that he will die because of disputes about him; his Mother is to suffer as well because of this death. That Joseph and Mary were amazed at Simeon's words seems impossible given all that had been revealed to them in the previous stories about their child, but the truth is that no revelation intended to make thoroughly understandable what would happen in future events. Thus, though Mary knew that Jesus was Son of God did not mean she could easily anticipate all that would happen to Jesus and to herself. Knowledgeable as she was at any one moment, there were always new, unexpected things to learn - she was known principally, not for her intelligence, but for her trust in whatever would come to her from God. The appearance of Anna, called a prophetess and a faithful Israelite (both meant to fill Luke's reader with continued faith), speaks well of Jesus; her presence, in the Court of the Women (Men had their own Court for prayer), is seen by many exegetes as Luke's attempt to balance prophecy by a man (Simeon) with that of the woman (Anna). Luke concludes with a brief summary. An important note is that the 'wisdom' of Jesus is best understood as 'learned wisdom'; i.e. Jesus the adult was very well-versed in the OT, and learned his wisdom from his childhood. That he did what he did reflects the truth that, though divine, he was like us in all things (e.g. study and piety), except sin. Zebulun and Naphtali, 2 of the 12 sons of Jacob, were the founders of tribes that carried their names for centuries. These tribes were established in what was later (and now is) Galilee. The tribes of Zebulun and Naphtali do not take up all the territory of Galilee in Jesus' time, but they do include such places associated with Jesus such as Nazareth, Cana and Capernaum; Zebulun's eastern border is the Sea of Galilee. It is Matthew who, as so very often, finds the appropriate words of God in the Jewish Scriptures to fit the 'Jesus situation' and who, believing in Jesus, affirms that this Scripture, when fully understood, applied to Jesus.

Light is one of those primordial images used to counter the human experience of ignorance, doubt and confusion, losing one's way in life, and death. Thus, when Matthew moves to present Jesus (fresh from his baptism and 40 days in the desert), it seemed most fitting to prepare the reader to understand Jesus: he is the light meant to bring wisdom and life - nothing short of that. Logically, then, the only proper response to a call to total following from this Person is to follow totally. Our Gospel asks us to consider the baptism of Jesus from the viewpoint of the Evangelist John. This Gospel passage means, in a minor key, to separate Jesus from John, and, in a major key, to begin the testimonies that run throughout the Fourth Gospel: Jesus is the divine Son of God. Yet, he affirms Jesus' divinity on every page. Finally, John presents Jesus as divine, not to convert non-believers, but to strengthen those already baptized and believing. Gospel

Each Gospel has its own way of dealing with Jesus' experience of baptism by John. The fact that each Gospel does speak of this event shows its great importance for understanding the life of Jesus to follow in the rest of the Gospel. It is significant that this story be the first event in the public life of Jesus for a reason we will see soon. The Matthean baptism story is divisible into two parts: the dialogue between Jesus and John and the revelation, in two forms, from heaven. It is difficult to prove that John would have been reluctant to baptize Jesus, for it is difficult to see, from these chapters leading up to this story, how John would have known that Jesus was so much greater than he. It seems more reasonable to appeal to two elements which could produce our story. First, Jewish rules of interpretation allow the Jewish Matthew to present events in their fullest meaning. Second, Christian faith, for many years before Matthew wrote (c. 85AD), worked out the correct relationship between John and Jesus, a relationship which was not so clear when John, the same age as Jesus , was preaching repentance like Jesus. However, we do know from Luke's account that on Mary's greeting of Elizabeth, the child in her womb leapt for joy. Faith and the mystical meaning of that exchange may be reason for John's statement that Jesus should baptize him. It is allowed then that faith and the 'rules' of the time express the truth of an event, though the data given are not actual; such is the effect of faith over the limitations of facts.

This Gospel suggests the problem about why Jesus would seek baptism; after all, faith has shown for years that Jesus was 'like us in all things but sin'. The solution to this problem lies in the purpose of baptism; it is a public act, using water (the symbol in Israel of cleanliness and life), to manifest the baptized's intention of doing the will of God. Such was the purpose of Jesus in coming to John - not that Jesus needed to repent, but he wished to manifest in a public way his intention to obey his Father. It can also be said that Jesus, by being baptized by John, encourages others to have faith in John and do the same.

The revelational aspect of Matthew's story is expressed by an act and a statement. First, there is the story of Sodom and Gomorrah, when those two cities perished for their sins with the people in no way aware of the judgment they should face. Swift was the punishment, upon those who were least ready for judgment. Second, Jesus pictures two men in a field, two women grinding at the mill. The suddenness of God's judgment will remove in a single swoop one of the two men and one of the two women. The image Jesus wants here is that the Judge, i.e. the Son of Man, will leave behind (for punishment) those who would fail the final judgment, and accepts into the kingdom those who are at every moment ready for judgment. Third, the daily awareness of possible theft always keeps one ready and alert for the thief; this is above all the case when owner of a house knows when the thief is coming: who would not be prepared?!? In various ways Jesus argues the wisdom of readiness for judgment. The judgment will come swiftly, at a moment not foreseen. If one fails to realize this fact, he will end up with a judgment he could have avoided, if he had readied himself for the 'anytime coming' of the Son of Man. If only he be ready, alert, he can be one of those 'taken' by the Son of Man, and not one of those 'left behind'. One is wise only if he is prepared for the Lord; to know that he could come at any time, and yet not be ready for him, is foolishness.

This Gospel is taken from a late chapter of Luke's Gospel, and is only a section of a much longer discourse of Jesus about the 'end time', a subject very much on the mind of Israelites of Jesus' time; indeed, most prophets over the centuries were preoccupied by the fact that some day God must right all wrongs, and when He does, evil in all its forms will be swept away for those who are pleasing to Yahweh. The Church puts this teaching of Jesus as a first consideration, as one prepares for the coming of the Lord on Christmas Day. One knows the day and the hour now, and so, the Church urges everyone to be prepared for God's entry into our world.

Gospel

In our Gospel this weekend, Jesus is on the cross. Because of the title over his head on the upright wooden beam, "Jesus of Nazareth, King of the Jews", the crucifying soldiers (of the Roman army, but not necessarily Roman citizens) mockingly address the crucified as 'King' and suggest that he do what would be expected of a king: exercise his power to free himself from this imminent death. Actually, the jeering Roman soldiers think of 'king' in their own terms: kings or emperors they have heard of in the Mediterranean countries of their time. For a Jew, however, 'king' meant not only one with supreme power, but also a person who was wise and holy, i.e. one who has God on his side. Ever since the time of David (most notably), Jews, except for a short period of time, knew only kingship. In particular, they knew the value of their kings, for the people's well-being and happiness was directly dependent on the power, wisdom and holiness of their kings. Should the king be weak in any of these three categories, his people suffered greatly. Indeed, when Israel, in its many times of suffering, looked for help from its covenant Partner, Israel thought in terms of the most perfectly powerful, wise and holy king possible, one who could overcome all dangers. Because the official act by which a person becomes king is an anointing with oil, he got the title Messiah, for Messiah in Hebrew means 'anointed' (and in Greek it means Christos). Thus, when Jesus entered into his public ministry, the people were already primed to look for a Messiah, a King, who could save them from all evil - for some, this saving meant a saving from Roman domination in particular. Though the charges against Jesus as he stood before the Jewish authorities did not include 'claiming to be the king', when Jesus was presented to Pilate, the most serious charge was that Jesus disturbed the peace Rome demanded and pretended to be the people's king; indeed, many followed Jesus not only because he seemed like a prophet from God, but also because they saw and heard from Jesus made them think of him as the promised king, Son of King David, his ancestor.

The criminals crucified with Jesus are Jewish; thus, they do not think of Jesus as king in the secular, gentile manner; the one ridicules him as one deserving of death, not as the Christ or Messiah. The other thinks of Jesus as his Messiah, who will rule with such power and wisdom and as friend of God that he will save from all threats to happiness. His faith in the identity of Jesus means that Jesus, king, does have a kingdom; the present circumstances argue that Jesus will enter his kingdom only after he dies. The 'good criminal' has every hope that Jesus will receive him into Jesus' kingdom where he rules and provides perfect happiness. 'Paradise' is a term, in origin referring to an enclosed, controlled space, but in time referring to a garden (enclosed) which flourishes (no weeds or ugliness here, just beauty, life, peace). It was not too many years after the passing of Jesus that his followers, because they claimed that the best way to define Jesus was to say he was 'Messiah' or 'King' or 'Anointed', became known as 'Christians', those in allegiance to Christ, King, Messiah, the Anointed One; their future will be in the kingdom of God.

Gospel

We are two weeks away from Advent, the beginning of a new liturgical, church year. This Sunday and the Sunday following excerpt sayings of Jesus about a concern of all Israel in his lifetime, namely, the end of this world as we know it. These readings coincide with Luke's attention to the final days of Jesus' own life: he is finally in Jerusalem, to die and then to live forever. It is always good to keep in mind that Luke has thought it valuable to repeat for his audience of 80-85AD what Jesus had said to his own audience some fifty years earlier; Jesus' words had meaning, Luke thinks, for his own Christian group. Note first that this story starts with an engaging pair of questions from people: when will the destruction of the Temple (Jerusalem) happen and what about all the things that are to happen before the end of this world; it is evident that the destruction of the Temple is considered by the people as one of the events which brings this world to an end. Jesus speaks here as a teacher of wisdom, turning the admiration of people at the site of the glorious Temple into a sombre warning of its total destruction. One might think he is speaking merely from an historical point of view; rather, he is, like the people, seeing the destruction of the Temple as a part of God's plan to end this world. Luke does not have Jesus dwell on the Temple's destruction. He knows that, at the time of his writing, that the Temple had been destroyed for at least ten years. Rather, Luke has Jesus move to remark about the 'other things' about which he was asked, especially in their relation to final destruction of the world; this concern to recognize the future signs of the approaching end is more important than the past events signalling this approach.

Luke knows that over the years since Jesus' death many people have claimed to be able to predict the end of the world. The usual approach to these predictions is to clothe the future in the same or similar terms which the ancient Hebrew prophets had so often, in their own times, used to speak about the coming justice of God and final punishment of Israel's enemies. Luke's concern, however, is not to have Jesus predict the timing or types of signs which precede and announce the coming end; rather, he denies the significance given to wars and other sufferings (all part of the expectations of Israel) and warns the disciples not to be tricked by them: the end is not near, though there be sufferings usually identified as forecasting that the end is at hand. What Luke attends to is the challenge, through all sufferings (especially persecutions for the faith with which his audience is very familiar), that Christians not deny their faith in Christ. The 'end-time', so much on the minds of Jesus' audience, is not on Luke's mind here: rather, what moves him is the desire to encourage fidelity in a world of trial and persecution, no matter how close or far is the end of the world. The words of Jesus Luke relays to his audience speak of terrible sufferings, even the betrayal by relatives and friends. Before these things happen we will be handed over to synagogues and prison because of Jesus' name. We must not prepare a defense beforehand, for Jesus himself will give us a wisdom against our adversaries they will be powerless to resist or refute. Luke reminds his audience that to be faithful to God's word is important, then God will look after those who persevere.

But, as is usual with Luke, concentration ends, not on negative experiences, but on virtue, here the virtues of perseverance and trust, confidence that the love God has for His People will overcome any and all challenges to faith and the obedience it demands.

Gospel

The Sadducees are best known to us for their denial of an afterlife or resurrection from the dead and of heavenly rewards and punishments. Their views were buttressed by their conviction that divine revelation is to be found only in the first five books of the OT, the Mosaic Torah, the Pentateuch - and only through a most conservative reading of those books. The rest of the OT is valuable, but does not contain revelation. The Gospel story today is actually an argument against resurrection from the dead and offers Jesus' response to that argument, especially drawing truth from the very books in which the Sadducees say there is no teaching that we will rise from the dead. They believe they can prove Jesus is wrong about resurrection from the dead by virtue of a story, which if followed logically, makes the afterlife ridiculous, thereby showing through ridicule that there is no reason to say that we will rise to new life after death. Their argument is based on a law from the book of Leviticus; in it is stated that a woman, should she not have had a child before her husband dies, must be married by the deceased's 'brother-in-law' so that she can produce a child. The Sadduceean story creates the need for seven men to marry the woman so that she have a child. Note that what is at stake here is a law to insure the preservation of the people of Israel; it was the hope that every woman could contribute to this preservation. From their story the Sadducees draw, in question form, their conclusion: which of the seven will be her husband in this so-called life after death; after all, she had seven husbands on earth. Jesus answers that there will be no need for the preservation of the people of Israel in the next life, because there will be no death, and thus no need for a substitute to keep Israel in existence. Having children, in the narrow sense, is the means of overcoming death and keeping Israel alive; in the afterlife, like angels who are undying, there will be no death and so no need to have children. That Moses agrees with Jesus can be seen from a citation from the very books most cherished by Sadducees; thus, Jesus answers them in their own terms. Moses spoke of God as 'the God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob'. This saying does not say that God was the forefathers' God, but that He is their God; since He is their God, and since God is a God only of life, to say that He is God of the forefathers, even though they had died, is to say that they rose from the dead. God is not God of the dead, but of the living. Not only then do the Sadducees not understand the meaning of Scripture, but they do not understand who their God really is: all people, dead or alive, are always alive to Him. Why? because He loves them. The story is a challenge to Luke's readers to realize that nothing will separate them from God, not even death; Luke thinks the story is worth repeating, not just an historical event, but as truth valid for his own readers to embrace.

Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob are heroes of the Old Testament alive in Jesus. As members of the New Testament are we heroes living for/in Jesus? After death?

Gospel

As is so often the case with Luke's Gospel, it is the circumstances with which Luke introduces a story that give the key to understanding Jesus' intentions. To introduce Jesus' story we read this Sunday, Luke cites Jesus' own words: he who defined his public life as that of one sent to call people to repentance now speaks about those 'who were convinced of their own righteousness and despised everyone else'.

It means to illustrate the teaching that one should not glorify oneself while despising others; it falls to God, who alone knows the heart of the Pharisee and the tax-collector, to identify the one who is just in His sight, and who is not. Gospel

At this point in his presentation, Luke tells of a teaching of Jesus given by Jesus through means of a miracle he generously performs for ten lepers. Some interpreters find fault with Jesus in this story, because he must first be asked, and does not perform the miracle on his own. Such certainly was not the meaning Luke intended here. But this opinion does remind us that the daily purpose of Jesus was not miracle working, but calling people to repentance. There must be, in Luke's thought, the right balance between a Jesus who can and does work miracles, even forgiving sins, and his calling as one who should ask all for repentance. Though this Gospel is filled with miracles, signs of the birth of the kingdom of God in Israel, Luke knows that, when all is said and done, Jesus has come to call sinners to repentance.

Literarily, the miracle is not described; we only know that somewhere it occurred - an indication that it is not the main point at issue here. Important are the instructions of Jesus, that these cured people should present show themselves to a priest or priests in the Jerusalem Temple area. Why should they do this? Because the Mosaic Law makes the word of the priest the assurance to the rest of Israel that 'what was unclean is now clean' and will no longer be a threat to the purity of the worship of God; if there is no priestly assurance, there is no acceptance of the 'unclean' by the worshipping people at its liturgies. The priest does not cure; he authenticates the truth of a cure and subsequent purity.

Jesus cannot tolerate the fact that only one leper, cured, returned to him to thank God. Some scholars see in this a polemic that Israel is ungrateful for so much, that the non-Jew will be willing to admit the action of God in his life - a prelude to the spread of Christian discipleship beyond Israel to the Gentiles. This grateful non-Jew is a Samaritan, and so in principle a determined enemy of Israel, who lives between Galilee and Judea. Samaritans expected One from God, whom they called Taheb, or Revealer, and not Messiah as in Israel. The Samaritan seems to think that God, not Jesus, cured him (though Luke thinks differently), but he is grateful to Jesus as the Revealer of the power of God and so returns to him gratefully. The final and crucial lesson of this story is now stated (and its wording is found often elsewhere in the Gospel): your faith has set you free. Faith in God, faith in Jesus - knowing who is God and what Jesus is, and embracing that knowledge with one's full will - that is freeing, saving. Gratitude follows upon blessing; salvation follows upon truth (who is the true God) and total love for and trust in Him.

Gospel

The words of Jesus in today's Gospel are part of a long speech Luke offers, in which he has presented the eloquent parable of the rich man and Lazarus in last week's Gospel. Jesus' point there was to underline his experience that so hard-hearted can one become that he will not repent even if someone returns from the dead to report the terrible suffering caused by selfishness. The words of Moses will be enough to bring about repentance, if one will only listen trustingly to his words.

It is shortly after this parable that Jesus speaks of faith, a faith so solid and undoubting and trusting that even the seemingly most improbable (uprooting of the mulberry tree) can happen. The reason the most unlikely will happen is not the faith itself, but the person in whom one has faith. Faith in the wrong person will yield nothing astounding.

In last week's text the rich man had faith in his wealth. The week before the steward who cheated on his master trusted in his worldly friends to help him save face. Even with faith we fall short of God's vision for us.

But, on the other hand, if the one believed in is truly believed in, then faith will fulfill its natural goal. The NT faith, built as mentioned above on the intellect, as reasonable and intelligible, believes the object of faith to be such as to be absolutely worthy of all trust.

Faith, then, is both belief in something as true, but also totally sure of the goodness of the reality it believes exists.

There are enough reasons, Jesus thinks, to move from what the mind can understand to embrace in certitude what we, by thinking alone, cannot affirm to be certain.

Introduction

All today's readings tell us to obey God's words , tell us how, and tell us why.
'Moses said to the people, 'if only you would heed the voice of the Lord, your God, and keep his commandments and statutes, that are written in the book of the law, when you return to the Lord, your God, with all your heart and all your soul."
Look outside of yourself at God's commandments given to Moses on the mountain. They are meant to be seen and learned by heart from the great prophet who received them from a conversation with God. Once we become conscious of God's words we have internalized them and are meant to keep them. Of course, our freedom is involved here; we can accept or reject God's commands. How many of us have really thought in our enthusiasm for freedom, that, that same freedom can take us to hell? That is, away from God and his love and glory. God's contact with Moses and God's commands are to show us through our senses that the God of Israel is real. He lives, he talks, he communicates, he has power and gives commands to those he favors and reveals himself to. This word of God is not in the sky or across the sea for someone to seek and bring back to us. It is close, once conscious of it, it remains in our hearts and on our lips; we have only to carry it out. Make it real, to exist, by our actions and pronouncements, and God becomes more real in our world.

Gospel

A scholar of the law thought that he would test Jesus with the question, 'Teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?" Jesus responded, 'What is written in the law? how do you read it?" The man answered correctly and Jesus approved his response, "Do this and you will live." The scholar wanted to test Jesus further by asking who is his neighbor. The Gospel text in Luke gives Jesus an opportunity to educate the scholar and us. He does this by giving us the parable of the Good Samaritan. In it Jesus moves from the world of the spiritual to the world of the physical, from theory to practice. The good neighbor is the one who shows mercy to the victim of the robbers. The scholar answered correctly of this also, so Jesus told him to, 'Go and do likewise." The good neighbor performs good works.

Genesis 14:18-20; Psalm 110:1, 2, 3, 4

Introduction

In today's first reading the traditional king of Salem, Melchizedek, blessed Abraham by God. He then blessed God who had delivered Abraham from the hands of his foes. Abraham gave Melchizedek one tenth of his booty, part of his due as king of Salem. This meeting was an introduction of our father in faith to Jerusalem which later became the spiritual center of Israel. Jerusalem would later become the city of king David who had saved his people from their enemies. Later still, Jesus Christ will save Jerusalem and the world from their spiritual enemies. In doing so Jesus reveals himself as king of heaven and earth residing in the heavenly Jerusalem.

Living Memory Today

How do we understand living memory today, what are its signs, its reality. I suggest to you that it is contained in the trauma of our times as Christ's was contained in his times. The signs were first noticed after W.W.I when troops returned home 'shell shocked." They were different from the men who left wives and loved ones to fight the war. The same phenomenon happened after W.W.II, Korea, and Vietnam. These men and were traumatized and carried in their enlivened mind and imagination the horrors of war. In the safety of their homes they were reliving the memories of war. Traumatic conditions spread into the cultural and social arena in the form of domestic violence, abortion, the sex trade, abuse of all sorts, drugs. Now one need not go to war to suffer from PTSD (Post Traumatic Stress Disorder); it can be had on the home front. The garden variety of such trauma is contained in 'the family baggage." Too many people in the world today have these kinds of living traumatic memories. God, it seems to me, has allowed us to arrive at this point of development so as to understand the meaning of 'living memory." Those who suffer these conditions have traumatic memories as their constant companions and will confirm that they are living. They are meant to lead us back to God the Father as his sufferings led Jesus home to the Father.

Do this in memory of me, brothers and sisters, may eventually come to mean do this in memory of us, but we have yet to pass in death. For the present, as members of Christ's Mystical Body on earth we participate in his active memory of dying and rising until he comes again.

Gospel

Jesus spoke to the crowds about the kingdom of God, and he healed those who needed to be cured -- actions by a man of princely power and might who knew what he was about. As the day drew to a close the apostles asked Jesus to dismiss the crowds that they might find something to eat at the nearby villages. Words of apostles with no princely power or might. Jesus had told them to give the crowd some food themselves. They were unable to do so as they did not have money to buy enough food for the crowd. A boy in the crowd had five loaves and a couple of fish. Jesus took them and looking up to heaven he said the blessing, broke them and gave them to the disciples to distribute to the crowds. This action bears a remarkable resemblance to that of Abraham before Melchizedek. Abraham acknowledged Melchizedek and received God's blessing. Jesus acknowledged the power of God and the crowd were fed. Jesus will pay our tithes to God for us later.

Reflection

The living memory of Jesus in the Eucharist keeps us alive today without our permission. We are meant to befriend him in our living memory on earth that we may live with him consciously and knowingly in eternity.

Gospel John 16:12-15

Today's Gospel from John is a brief resume of Jesus' farewell discourse dealt with in chapters fourteen and sixteen. It combines the teaching of the Father for the disciples of Jesus, to keep the word of God as preached by Jesus and enlightened by the Holy Spirit. Jesus has many things to tell his disciples but they cannot hear them.

The gruesome details of Jesus' passion, death are too much for them to support, and his glorious resurrection is too incredible for them to understand without seeing him alive. They are not mature enough psychologically and spiritually to withstand events which they cannot conceive. However, when the Spirit of truth comes he will lead you to the complete truth, since he will not be speaking of his own accord, but will say only what he has been told; and he will reveal to you the things to come.
"He will glorify me, since all he reveals to you will be taken from what is mine.
Everything the Father has is mine; that is why I said: all he reveals to you will be taken from what is mine."
Jesus is teaching them what they need to know before he leaves earth.
The lesson is that they must keep the Father's word in order to gain eternal life.
The Father's word and that of Jesus are the same, for Jesus is the Word of God Incarnate.
The Holy Spirit who has been with the Father from the beginning will enlighten the disciples.
Jesus is passing on this knowledge of God to his disciples through the role of the persons in the Holy Trinity.
As human beings they must receive it in faith by keeping the words of Jesus and the Father. The Holy Spirit's role is to enlighten us as he is the Spirit of the Father and the Son and knows them intimately as Spirit. Christ's followers are meant to imitate the Trinitarian relationship by loving God, self, and neighbor through a community enlivened by God's Holy Spirit.
As the Father sent Jesus on a mission to earth to reveal him, So Jesus and the Father send the Holy Spirit on mission to enlighten and guide his followers.
The mission of Christ's followers is contained in their unity as members of the Church.
The Church is missioned to the whole world to reveal the words of Jesus and the Father.
